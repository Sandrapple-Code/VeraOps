## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 56-year-old male engineer with a unique, extensive surgical history involving a historical chest injury and subsequent complex hardware extraction who was admitted to the Coronary Care Unit following an emergency department presentation for acute-onset retrosternal chest pain. The patient reported that while working in his laboratory under high stress, he experienced the sudden onset of a tight, crushing pressure sensation located directly beneath his sternum, radiating up into his left jaw and accompanied by immediate cold sweats and noticeable heart racing. He attempted to use his home anxiety medication without any relief of the physical discomfort. Upon assessment in the emergency room, his initial blood pressure was elevated at 178/102 mmHg, and his heart rate was fluctuating erratically between 95 and 110 beats per minute. Continuous telemetry monitoring caught an immediate 4-beat run of monomorphic non-sustained ventricular tachycardia, prompting high-acuity cardiac intervention. Physical exam revealed a well-healed, extensive vertical mid-sternotomy scar, a regular heart rhythm apart from occasional premature ventricular contractions, clear lung fields, and intact peripheral pulses. Initial serial troponin values hovered at the upper limit of normal but did not show an acute upward evolution. However, his 12-lead electrocardiogram revealed significant, progressive lateral T-wave inversions that were entirely new compared to his historical baselines. Due to the dangerous combination of unstable ischemic symptoms, acute ECG changes, and malignant ventricular ectopy, he was admitted directly to the CCU for aggressive medical stabilization and invasive stratifying procedures.
