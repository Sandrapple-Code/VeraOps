## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 17-year-old adolescent male with no significant past medical history who presented to the pediatric emergency department accompanied by his aunt due to acute-onset severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, and a noticeable altered state of lethargy. The aunt reported that over the preceding three to four weeks, the patient had been exhibiting excessive thirst, consuming large quantities of water, and waking up multiple times throughout the night to urinate. Despite this massive fluid intake and an increased appetite, he had lost approximately 6 kilograms of body weight and had become increasingly weak. On physical examination, the patient appeared severely dehydrated with dry, parched mucous membranes, sunken eyes, and delayed skin turgor. His vital signs were significant for tachypnea at 28 breaths per minute with deep, labored chest excursions characteristic of Kussmaul breathing, a tachycardic heart rate of 122 beats per minute, and a stable blood pressure of 108/66 mmHg. A distinct, sweet, sweet-acetone fruity odor was easily detectable on his breath. His abdomen was diffusely tender to palpation, mimicry of an acute surgical abdomen, but remained soft with no guarding or rebound tenderness. A point-of-care fingerstick blood glucose was critically elevated at 542 mg/dL. Immediate venous blood gas and metabolic panels confirmed a profound metabolic acidosis with a large anion gap and heavy serum and urinary ketones, fulfilling the diagnostic criteria for severe diabetic ketoacidosis secondary to previously undiagnosed type 1 diabetes mellitus.

## Initial Clinical Presentation
- **Chief Complaint:** Polyuria, polydipsia, profound weight loss (6 kg over 3 weeks), severe generalized abdominal pain, nausea, multiple episodes of emesis, deep rapid respirations (Kussmaul breathing), fruity breath odor.
- **Initial Diagnosis:** New-Onset Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus presenting with Severe Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

## Admission Orders & Treatment Plan
Immediate admission to the pediatric step-down unit, aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation with 0.9% Normal Saline boluses followed by a continuous infusion of regular insulin at 0.1 units/kg/hour. Continuous monitoring of hourly capillary blood glucose, serum electrolytes (especially potassium and phosphorus), and venous blood gas parameters. Once blood glucose drops below 250 mg/dL, dextrose will be added to the intravenous fluids (D5 0.45% NS) to allow continued insulin infusion until the anion gap closes and acidosis resolves. Slow transition to a subcutaneous basal-bolus insulin regimen.
