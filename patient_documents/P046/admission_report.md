## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 2-year-old male toddler who was brought to the pediatric emergency department by his parents at 02:00 AM due to a sudden onset of alarming respiratory distress and a loud cough. The father noted that the child had mild coryza during the day, but woke up gasping for air with a cough that sounded like a barking seal. Upon assessment in the emergency room, the child was highly agitated, tachycardic at 142 bpm, tachypneic at 34 breaths per minute, and maintaining an oxygen saturation of 89% on room air. Clear, harsh inspiratory stridor was audible without a stethoscope whenever the child was quiet, and escalated dramatically when he cried. Marked suprastrunal, intercostal, and subcostal retractions were present, indicating an advanced work of breathing and a high risk for respiratory fatigue. A soft tissue neck radiograph demonstrated a beautiful, classic steeple sign confirming severe subglottic swelling from parainfluenza croup. Given the severe nature of the stridor at rest and the requirement for active nebulized epinephrine and intravenous steroids due to an oral liquid steroid intolerance, he was formally admitted for inpatient pediatric observation.

## Initial Clinical Presentation
- **Chief Complaint:** Harsh, inspiratory stridor audible at rest, a loud barking metallic cough resembling a seal, hoarse crying, significant suprastrunal and intercostal retractions, low-grade fever.
- **Initial Diagnosis:** Acute Laryngotracheobronchitis (Severe Croup), Secondary Hypoxia

## Admission Orders & Treatment Plan
Immediate administration of nebulized racemic epinephrine (0.5mL of 2.25% solution) to rapidly reduce subglottic mucosal edema; due to his severe vomiting reaction to oral liquid steroids, a single dose of intravenous Dexamethasone (0.6 mg/kg) was delivered after securing IV access; administration of cool, humidified supplemental oxygen via 'blow-by' tubing; close tracking of respiratory rates and work of breathing.
