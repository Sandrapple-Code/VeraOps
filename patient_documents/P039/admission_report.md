## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 16-year-old adolescent male with an established history of childhood asthma who was brought to the pediatric emergency department by his mother due to a 12-hour history of rapidly progressive shortness of breath, severe chest tightness, and audible wheezing that failed to respond to multiple home rescue treatments. The mother noted that he had a mild upper respiratory infection earlier in the week. Upon presentation, he was tachypneic at 30 breaths per minute, tachycardic at 124 bpm, and maintaining an oxygen saturation of 89% on room air. He was utilizing his accessory neck muscles for respiration and could only speak in short, broken phrases. Auscultation of the chest revealed globally restricted air movement with tight, diffuse, bilateral inspiratory and expiratory wheezing. Initial emergency labs were obtained, which revealed a completely unexpected, significant elevation in cardiac Troponin I at 1.25 ng/mL, raising immediate concern for an inflammatory myocarditis complicating his presentation. An electrocardiogram showed sinus tachycardia with diffuse repolarization abnormalities. Given his unique history of severe tachycardia and hypokalemia with continuous albuterol therapy, he was admitted to the pediatric intermediate care service for spaced, closely monitored bronchodilator treatments, systemic steroids, and continuous telemetry.

## Initial Clinical Presentation
