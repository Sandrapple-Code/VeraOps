## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 58-year-old male with a significant history of neglected type 2 diabetes, chronic tobacco use, and advanced peripheral vascular disease who was emergently admitted to the cardiology service following a presentation for an acute hypertensive crisis and severe respiratory distress. The patient reported that he was resting when he suddenly developed an overwhelming sensation of suffocation, preventing him from lying flat and causing him to cough up small amounts of pink, frothy secretions, accompanied by a blinding headache and blurred vision. Upon arrival via EMS, he was in profound respiratory distress, orthopneic, diaphoretic, tachypneic at 28 breaths per minute, and severely hypertensive with a blood pressure of 224/128 mmHg. His oxygen saturation was 82% on room air. Physical exam was remarkable for loud bilateral pulmonary crackles extending to the upper lung fields, a prominent S4 gallop, and severe chronic skin changes in the lower extremities including a dry varus deformity of the right foot and a 2cm ischemic ulcer over his left lateral malleolus. Laboratory evaluations confirmed a markedly elevated NT-proBNP and acute kidney injury. A chest radiograph confirmed severe acute pulmonary congestion. Given the acute target-organ damage manifesting as flash pulmonary edema, he was immediately placed on non-invasive mechanical ventilation and started on titratable intravenous vasodilators.
