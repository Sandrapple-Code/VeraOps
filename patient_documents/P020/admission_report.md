## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 55-year-old male with a history of chronic bronchial asthma and essential hypertension who presented to the emergency department complaining of a four-day history of rapidly progressive respiratory symptoms. He reported a severe, painful cough productive of an abundant volume of thick, foul-smelling, greenish-blue sputum, accompanied by high spiking fevers, shaking chills, and a sharp, stabbing left-sided pleuritic chest pain that became intolerable with deep inspiration. On physical examination, the patient was in moderate respiratory distress, tachypneic at 26 breaths per minute, febrile to 39.3 degrees Celsius, and tachycardic at 108 beats per minute, with an oxygen saturation of 86% on room air. Auscultation of the chest was remarkable for profoundly decreased breath sounds and coarse, bubbling crackles over the entire left lung base, accompanied by diffuse, high-pitched expiratory wheezing throughout both the right lung and left upper lung fields, indicating a significant secondary asthmatic bronchospasm triggered by the acute pulmonary infection. Laboratory data revealed a severe leukocytosis and significant hypoxemia on room air. A chest radiograph demonstrated a dense, heavy consolidation filling the left lower lobe. Given the aggressive nature of the lobar pneumonia, the presence of pseudomonal-appearing sputum, and the patient's underlying history of asthma, he was immediately admitted to the pulmonology service for targeted intravenous antibiotic therapy and close respiratory tracking.
