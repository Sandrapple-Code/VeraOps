## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 68-year-old female with an extensive history of ischemic cardiomyopathy, type 2 diabetes mellitus, and stage 3 chronic kidney disease who presented to the emergency department complaining of progressively worsening shortness of breath over the past week. She reported that her dyspnea, which was previously limited to heavy exertion, had progressed to occurring with minimal ambulation and was accompanied by severe orthopnea requiring her to sleep upright in a chair. Upon initial physical examination in the emergency department, she was found to be tachypneic with a respiratory rate of 24 breaths per minute, hypoxic with an oxygen saturation of 88% on room air, hypertensive at 165/94 mmHg, and tachycardic with a heart rate fluctuating between 102 and 110 beats per minute. Auscultation of the lungs revealed diffuse bilateral pulmonary crackles extending halfway up both lung fields, along with distant heart sounds and a prominent S3 gallop. Marked 3+ pitting edema was noted bilaterally up to the mid-calf region. Initial laboratory workup was significant for a markedly elevated NT-proBNP of 4,200 pg/mL, indicating severe myocardial wall stress consistent with acute decompensated heart failure. Her serum creatinine was elevated at 1.6 mg/dL, representing a mild acute-on-chronic kidney injury likely secondary to venous congestion. Electrocardiogram demonstrated sinus tachycardia with old left bundle branch block but no acute ST-segment or T-wave abnormalities. A bedside chest radiograph confirmed central pulmonary congestion, cardiomegaly, and small bilateral pleural effusions. Given the patient's critical volume overload and respiratory compromise, the decision was made to admit her to the Cardiology sub-acute service for aggressive intravenous diuretic therapy, continuous telemetry, and close metabolic tracking.
