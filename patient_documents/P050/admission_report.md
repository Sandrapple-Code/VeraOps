## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 24-year-old female biochemistry student with no significant past medical history apart from a severe viral-suspect diarrheal illness three weeks ago who was admitted to the neurology service following an emergency room presentation for rapidly progressive, ascending bodily weakness. The patient reported that forty-eight hours prior, she noticed a strange tingling sensation in her toes, which quickly advanced into a heavy weakness in both legs, making it difficult to climb stairs and ultimately preventing her from standing unassisted this morning. She also noted a new difficulty in closing her eyes fully and a mild shortness of breath. On physical examination, she was afebrile and normotensive, though mildly tachypneic at 20 breaths per minute. Neurological evaluation revealed an alert female with bilateral lower motor neuron facial nerve palsies (facial diplegia). Motor strength was reduced to 2/5 in the proximal and distal lower extremities bilaterally, with a flaccid tone and a complete absence of deep tendon reflexes (0/4) at the knees and ankles. Sensation to vibration and light touch was mildly diminished in a stocking distribution. A lumbar puncture confirmed a classic albuminocytological dissociation, establishing a definitive high-risk diagnosis of Guillain-Barré syndrome and requiring immediate admission for immunomodulatory therapy and respiratory tracking.
