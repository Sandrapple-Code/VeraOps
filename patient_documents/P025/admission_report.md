## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 47-year-old male prominent district attorney with a medical history of hypertension and bipolar disorder who was rushed to the emergency department as a level 1 trauma alert following a severe assault involving the intentional throwing of a highly corrosive acid chemical agent onto his body. Upon presentation, the patient was in extreme agony, tachypneic at 32 breaths per minute, tachycardic at 128 beats per minute, and hypertensive at 174/104 mmHg. Physical examination revealed catastrophic, deep full-thickness chemical burns encompassing the entire left lateral half of his face, left ear, left neck, and the anterior aspect of his left shoulder and upper arm, estimating approximately 20% total body surface area. The burned skin on the left side appeared charred, leathery, and completely anesthetic to pinprick, while the surrounding edges were exquisitely painful and erythematous. Most critically, the patient exhibited significant inspiratory stridor, a hoarse muffled voice, a continuous brassy cough producing thick carbonaceous sputum, and visible soot deposition within his oral cavity and nares, indicating an acute, life-threatening inhalation injury. Due to the high risk of immediate airway loss from rapidly expanding supraglottic edema, a code airway was called, and the patient was successfully intubated via rapid sequence induction. Large-bore intravenous access was secured, aggressive fluid resuscitation based on the Parkland formula was commenced, and he was transferred directly to the ICU.
