## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 49-year-old male with a significant baseline history of Stage 4 chronic kidney disease and poorly controlled type 2 diabetes who was brought to the emergency department via advanced life support ambulance after experiencing the sudden onset of crushing, unrelenting retrosternal chest pain that began while he was at rest. The pain was described as an intolerable pressure sensation, associated with radiation into his left jaw and upper extremity, accompanied by profound drenching diaphoresis, severe nausea, and a rapid progression to extreme shortness of breath. Upon arrival by EMS, the patient was found to be hypotensive with a blood pressure of 78/42 mmHg, tachycardic at 112 beats per minute, and severely hypoxic with an oxygen saturation of 78% on a non-rebreather mask, demonstrating clinical signs of florid cardiogenic shock and acute decompensated pulmonary edema. Physical examination revealed cold, cyanotic, poorly perfused distal extremities, distant heart sounds, and coarse bubbling crackles throughout all lung fields bilaterally. A 12-lead electrocardiogram was immediately performed, demonstrating hyperacute ST-segment elevations in the precordial leads V1-V5, establishing the diagnosis of an acute extensive anterior wall STEMI. Furthermore, the ECG revealed widened QRS complexes and peaked T-waves highly suggestive of concurrent severe hyperkalemia. Laboratory analysis confirmed a critical serum potassium of 6.7 mEq/L and a markedly elevated troponin. Given his critical state, emergency intubation was performed in the ER, and the patient was immediately transferred to the Intensive Care Unit for urgent mechanical circulatory support device placement and multi-inotropic therapy.
