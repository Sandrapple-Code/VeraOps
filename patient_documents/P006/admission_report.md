## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 34-year-old male who presented to the emergency department complaining of sudden-onset, severe, unremitting right upper quadrant abdominal pain that commenced approximately four hours after consuming a high-fat meal. The patient described the pain as sharp, localized to the right upper quadrant, and radiating intensely to his right shoulder blade. The pain was accompanied by persistent nausea and three episodes of non-bilious, non-bloody vomiting at home. On physical examination, the patient was in moderate distress, diaphoretic, and febrile to 38.1 degrees Celsius. His blood pressure was 142/88 mmHg and heart rate was 98 beats per minute. Abdominal examination revealed normal bowel sounds but profound, exquisite tenderness to deep palpation in the right upper quadrant, with an involuntary arrest of inspiration during palpation beneath the right costal margin, confirming a positive clinical Murphy's sign. There was no abdominal guarding or rebound tenderness. Laboratory evaluations were significant for a leukocytosis of 14,200 cells/uL with a neutrophil dominance, while liver function tests and pancreatic enzymes were entirely within normal limits, ruling out concurrent choledocholithiasis or acute biliary pancreatitis. An emergent point-of-care right upper quadrant ultrasound confirmed gallbladder wall thickening, gallstones, and pericholecystic fluid, completing the diagnostic criteria for acute calculous cholecystitis.

## Initial Clinical Presentation
