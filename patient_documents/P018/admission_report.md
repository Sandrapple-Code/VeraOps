## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 36-year-old male with an unremarkable past medical history who was emergently brought to the Level 1 Trauma Center via life-flight helicopter following a catastrophic industrial accident at a construction site where a heavy steel beam collapsed, crushing his left thorax and upper abdomen. Upon arrival in the trauma bay, the patient was in severe respiratory distress, cyanotic, diaphoretic, and demonstrating signs of profound hypovolemic shock with an initial blood pressure of 80/44 mmHg and a thready, tachycardic heart rate of 136 beats per minute. Physical examination revealed a large area of ecchymosis and chest wall deformity over the left hemithorax, with a highly visible paradoxical inward movement of the left chest wall during spontaneous inspiration, pathognomonic for a flail chest. Auscultation revealed completely absent breath sounds over the entire left lung field and dullness to percussion. The abdomen was rigid, severely distended, and exquisitely tender, with significant guarding localized to the left upper quadrant. A Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma (FAST) scan was immediately performed, demonstrating a large volume of free intraperitoneal fluid in the splenorenal recess. Large-bore peripheral intravenous lines were established, aggressive fluid resuscitation via the massive transfusion protocol was initiated, and a large-bore chest tube was urgently inserted into the left fifth intercostal space at the midaxillary line to decompress a life-threatening hemopneumothorax, yielding an immediate return of blood and air. The patient was stabilized sufficiently to undergo an emergent pan-computed tomography scan, which confirmed multi-segmental rib fractures, a massive hemopneumothorax, and a severe Grade III splenic injury with active contrast blush, requiring immediate surgical and radiological intervention.
