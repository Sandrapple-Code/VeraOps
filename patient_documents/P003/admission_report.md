## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 72-year-old female with a known history of chronic arterial hypertension and non-valvular atrial fibrillation who was brought to the emergency department after being found down by her husband. The husband reported that the patient was in her usual state of health until approximately 08:00 AM when she suddenly dropped a teacup, developed a severe droop on the right side of her face, and became completely unable to speak coherently, uttering only unintelligible sounds. Upon arrival via EMS at 09:15 AM, the patient's vital signs showed a blood pressure of 188/104 mmHg, heart rate of 112 beats per minute in an irregularly irregular rhythm, and an oxygen saturation of 97% on room air. Neurological examination revealed a profound global expressive aphasia, a dense right-sided facial droop, flaccid paralysis of the right upper extremity (0/5 strength), and significant weakness in the right lower extremity (2/5 strength), culminating in an initial NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS) score of 18. A code stroke was activated immediately. A non-contrast head CT was rapidly performed, ruling out any acute intracranial hemorrhage but demonstrating subtle early ischemic changes within the left hemisphere. A subsequent CT angiogram localized a dense occlusion within the M1 segment of the left middle cerebral artery. Because the patient had a documented history of severe gastrointestinal bleeding with aspirin and had admitted to missing multiple doses of her oral anticoagulant (Apixaban) over the preceding week, she was deemed an ideal candidate for emergent mechanical thrombectomy. The patient was taken urgently to the interventional neuroradiology suite where successful mechanical clot retrieval was performed, yielding complete recanalization of the vessel.
