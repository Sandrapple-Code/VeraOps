## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 61-year-old female fashion executive with a known history of severe carotid artery stenosis who was brought to the emergency department after collapsing suddenly at her office. Her daughter reported that the patient was speaking normally when she suddenly slurred her words, slumped heavily to the left side, and fell out of her chair. Upon arrival via EMS stroke alert at 14:15 PM, she was hypertensive at 182/96 mmHg and tachycardic at 102 bpm. Neurological examination revealed a profound right hemisphere syndrome: dense left-sided hemiplegia affecting her face, arm, and leg, severe left-sided spatial and sensory neglect, and profound anosognosia. Her NIH Stroke Scale score was critically high at 21. A non-contrast head CT ruled out any acute intracranial hemorrhage. A subsequent CT angiogram localized a severe, calcified occlusion at the terminus of the right internal carotid artery. Because she was within the ideal therapeutic window, she was taken emergently to the neuro-interventional radiology suite for urgent mechanical thrombectomy to rescue the ischemic penumbra. She was subsequently admitted to the specialized stroke service for critical management.
