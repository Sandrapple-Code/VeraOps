## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 5-year-old male child, the son of a physician colleague, who was brought to the pediatric emergency department presenting with a 24-hour history of severe, unrelenting watery diarrhea and intractable vomiting. The father reported that the child had been unable to tolerate any oral fluids, including small sips of water or electrolyte solution, for the past 12 hours, and had not produced a wet diaper or urinated since the previous evening. On physical examination, the child appeared noticeably lethargic, lying limply on the examination table, though he was responsive to verbal commands. His vital signs revealed tachycardia with a heart rate of 142 beats per minute, a mild low-grade fever of 37.8 degrees Celsius, and a blood pressure of 90/54 mmHg. Clinical signs of significant volume depletion were prominent, including dry and cracked oral mucous membranes, sunken orbits, prolonged capillary refill time of approximately 3 seconds, decreased skin turgor with slow recoil, and absent tear production when crying. The abdomen was soft, non-distended, with hyperactive bowel sounds in all quadrants, and mild diffuse tenderness without guarding or rigidity. Initial laboratory investigations demonstrated a significant elevation in Blood Urea Nitrogen at 28 mg/dL, indicating pre-renal azotemia, alongside a depleted serum potassium of 3.1 mEq/L and a low serum bicarbonate of 16 mEq/L. The stool sample was collected and sent for a comprehensive viral and bacterial pathogen panel. Based on the child's clinically significant dehydration, inability to tolerate oral intake, and electrolyte imbalances, he was admitted to the pediatric observation service for immediate intravenous rehydration and correction of hypokalemia.

## Initial Clinical Presentation
