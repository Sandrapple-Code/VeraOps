## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 41-year-old male test pilot with a history of hyperlipidemia who was emergently admitted to the Intensive Care Unit following a sudden cardiac arrest secondary to an acute myocardial infarction. His spouse reported that they were walking when he suddenly clutched his chest, complained of a tearing pressure behind his breastbone, and collapsed to the ground, becoming completely unresponsive and turning cyanotic. Bystander cardiopulmonary resuscitation was initiated immediately by a nearby citizen. Upon arrival of emergency medical services six minutes later, the patient was found to be in pulseless ventricular fibrillation. A single 200-Joule electrical shock was delivered, resulting in an immediate return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC). On presentation to the trauma bay, the patient was obtunded but opening his eyes to painful stimuli. His blood pressure was low at 84/48 mmHg, and his heart rate was 106 bpm. A post-arrest 12-lead ECG revealed catastrophic ST-segment elevations across the entire precordial distribution, indicating an acute anterior STEMI. A cardiac alert was activated instantly, and the patient was taken directly to the cardiac catheterization suite where an emergent percutaneous coronary intervention successfully opened a completely occluded left anterior descending artery.
