## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 67-year-old male with a history of hypertension who was admitted to the medicine service following an emergency department presentation for a severe left-sided headache and transient visual blackouts. The patient reported a two-month history of a deep, unremitting ache and profound stiffness in his bilateral shoulders and hips, which made it nearly impossible to get out of bed in the morning. Over the past three days, this was eclipsed by a new, intense, throbbing pain over his left temple, a painful tightening sensation in his jaw muscles that occurred halfway through chewing a meal, and two frightening episodes where the vision in his left eye went completely black for approximately thirty seconds. On physical examination, he was afebrile and hypertensive at 154/92 mmHg. Exquisite tenderness and a noticeably thickened, nodular, pulseless cord-like structure were present upon palpation of the left temporal artery. Active abduction of both arms was limited by bilateral shoulder girdle pain, though direct muscle strength was preserved. Laboratory screens revealed a critically elevated ESR of 105 mm/hr. Given the high risk of permanent, irreversible blindness secondary to ischemic optic neuropathy from Giant Cell Arteritis, he was immediately hospitalized for emergent high-dose intravenous pulse corticosteroid therapy.
