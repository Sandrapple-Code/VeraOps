## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 59-year-old male with a significant history of severe stage III Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease and a heavy 45 pack-year history of cigarette smoking who presented to the emergency room via direct referral from his primary care provider due to severe respiratory distress, acute hypoxemia, and high fevers at home. The patient reported a five-day history of a worsening productive cough producing thick, rust-colored purulent sputum, alongside sharp, right-sided pleuritic chest pain that exacerbated upon deep inspiration. On presentation, he was actively distressed, tachypleic at 28 breaths per minute, febrile to 39.2 degrees Celsius, tachycardic at 115 beats per minute, and hypoxic with an oxygen saturation of 82% on ambient room air. Physical exam was highly notable for diminished breath sounds and coarse crackles over the right lower lung base, diffuse expiratory wheezing throughout all other lung fields, and visible intercostal and supraclavicular muscle retractions indicating a high work of breathing. An urgent arterial blood gas analysis confirmed acute respiratory acidosis secondary to severe ventilation-perfusion mismatch and acute chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure. A chest radiograph demonstrated a dense, well-defined segmental consolidation filling the right lower lobe, highly characteristic of lobar bacterial pneumonia. Due to the high risk of rapid respiratory decompensation in a patient with limited baseline pulmonary reserve, he was immediately admitted to the respiratory step-down unit for aggressive intravenous antibiotic management, corticosteroid therapy, and intensive respiratory support.
