## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 43-year-old female with a background history of migraines and chronic iron deficiency anemia who was admitted to the neurology service following an emergency department presentation for a progressive, debilitating headache. The patient reported that the headache began approximately one week prior and differed completely from her baseline migraines, describing it as a constant, deep, crushing pressure that expanded to involve her entire head. The headache was markedly exacerbated by lying flat in bed, bending forward, or coughing, and was accompanied by transient episodes of complete visual blackouts lasting a few seconds, mild double vision, and multiple episodes of projectile vomiting. Upon initial physical exam, she was afebrile, with a blood pressure of 134/82 mmHg and a heart rate of 74 beats per minute. Neurological assessment revealed an alert and oriented female with a mild bilateral abducens nerve (CN VI) palsy causing restricted lateral gaze and subjective horizontal diplopia. Fundoscopic examination demonstrated bilateral optic disc swelling with blurred margins and flame-shaped hemorrhages, highly indicative of severe papilledema secondary to increased intracranial pressure. Laboratory workup was notable for an elevated D-Dimer and a microcytic anemia. An emergent non-contrast brain CT and subsequent contrast-enhanced magnetic resonance venogram (MRV) conclusively demonstrated an extensive thrombosis within the superior sagittal and left transverse venous sinuses. Given the high risk of venous infarction or intracranial hemorrhage, she was immediately hospitalized for therapeutic anticoagulation.

## Initial Clinical Presentation
- **Chief Complaint:** Severe, progressive, generalized headache described as 'the worst headache of her life', worse when lying flat and with coughing, associated with transient visual obscurations, mild horizontal diplopia, and severe nausea with vomiting.
- **Initial Diagnosis:** Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis (CVST), Intracranial Hypertension, Iron Deficiency Anemia

## Admission Orders & Treatment Plan
Immediate discontinuation of all estrogen-containing oral contraceptive medications; initiation of a continuous intravenous therapeutic Heparin infusion without a bolus, subsequently transitioning to oral anticoagulation with Warfarin; aggressive headache management using non-acetaminophen agents (intravenous Ketorolac and Topiramate); continuous monitoring for signs of worsening intracranial pressure, visual acuity checks, and serial neurological examinations.
