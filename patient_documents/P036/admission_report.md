## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 53-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol consumption who presented to the emergency department via police escort following a severe physical altercation at a local tavern. Upon arrival, the patient was alert but highly uncooperative and irritable, smelling strongly of alcohol and complaining of a severe throbbing headache. His vital signs were stable, with a blood pressure of 142/86 mmHg, heart rate of 88 bpm, and a normal respiratory rate. Physical examination revealed multiple deep, linear, clean-cut soft tissue lacerations across his anterior chest wall and the dorsal aspects of both forearms, with active capillary bleeding. Pronounced soft tissue ecchymosis and localized swelling were noted over his right zygomatic arch and occipital scalp. He displayed mild confusion and a brief period of post-traumatic amnesia regarding the exact details of the event, satisfying the criteria for a mild traumatic brain injury. Neurological evaluation was otherwise non-focal, with symmetric pupillary responses and intact gross motor function. Laboratory work confirmed an elevated blood alcohol level, while all other parameters were within normal functional limits. Given the extensive nature of the lacerations and the need for head injury observation alongside alcohol withdrawal tracking, he was placed in the ED observation unit.
