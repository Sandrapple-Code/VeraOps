## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is an 8-year-old female with a well-documented medical history of moderate persistent childhood asthma and atopic allergies who was brought to the pediatric emergency department by her parents at midnight due to rapidly worsening respiratory distress that did not respond to multiple home treatments of her rescue albuterol inhaler. The father stated that the patient developed mild upper respiratory symptoms including rhinorrhea and sneezing two days prior, followed by the acute onset of an intractable dry cough and audible wheezing that peaked in severity this evening. Upon physical assessment in the emergency department, the child appeared highly anxious, sitting upright in a tripod position, tachypneic with a respiratory rate of 38 breaths per minute, tachycardic with a heart rate of 134 beats per minute, and maintaining an oxygen saturation of 89% on room air. She exhibited profound intercostal, subcostal, and suprastrunal retractions along with pronounced nasal flaring, and was only capable of communicating in single, monosyllabic words due to extreme shortness of breath. Auscultation of the chest revealed globally diminished air entry with tight, diffuse, high-pitched inspiratory and expiratory wheezing throughout all lung fields. Capillary blood gas demonstrated a normal pCO2 of 39 mmHg, which in the setting of severe tachypnea and high work of breathing indicates impending respiratory muscle fatigue and a high risk for acute respiratory failure. A chest radiograph demonstrated significant pulmonary hyperinflation and low diaphragms consistent with severe bronchospasm, but cleared her of any secondary focal pneumonia or air leaks. Due to the diagnosis of status asthmaticus failing outpatient and initial emergency interventions, she was formally admitted to the pediatric inpatient floor for close monitoring and continuous medical optimization.
