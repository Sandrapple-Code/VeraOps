## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 41-year-old female with an established, complex medical history of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus and Antiphospholipid Syndrome who was admitted to the general medicine service following a direct referral from her rheumatologist due to clinical and laboratory signs indicating a severe systemic disease flare with renal involvement. The patient reported experiencing a severe, burning erythematous malar rash across her face for the past three weeks following accidental sun exposure, accompanied by worsening symmetric swelling, warmth, and severe pain in her bilateral wrists, metacarpophalangeal, and proximal interphalangeal joints. Furthermore, she noted the recent onset of profound, debilitating fatigue, a 5-kilogram weight gain over two weeks, and progressively worsening bilateral lower extremity edema up to her knees, alongside highly frothy urine. Upon admission, her vital signs were significant for an elevated blood pressure of 158/96 mmHg, a heart rate of 88 breaths per minute, and a temperature of 37.5 degrees Celsius. Physical exam was remarkable for a classic butterfly rash over her malar eminence, active synovitis with restricted range of motion in multiple hand joints, and 2+ pitting edema of the lower limbs. Initial laboratory evaluation revealed critical markers of an acute lupus flare: her anti-dsDNA titers were markedly elevated, and her serum C3 and C4 complement levels were profoundly reduced, indicating severe immune-complex mediated consumption. Most concerningly, her urinalysis showed heavy proteinuria and active cellular sediment, and her serum creatinine was acutely doubled from her baseline. A percutaneous ultrasound-guided renal biopsy was emergently performed on day 2, which confirmed the diagnosis of active Class IV (Diffuse Proliferative) Lupus Nephritis, necessitating immediate aggressive immunosuppressive therapy.
