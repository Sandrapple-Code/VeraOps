## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 29-year-old female with an extensive and high-risk medical history of severe persistent eosinophilic asthma, characterized by a history of mechanical ventilation due to status asthmaticus, who was admitted to the pulmonology service following an emergency department presentation for an acute, severe asthma flare. The patient stated that her symptoms were acutely triggered approximately six hours prior when she was exposed to feline dander at a residence. She immediately experienced intense chest tightness, an uncontrollable dry cough, and rapidly progressive shortness of breath that failed to respond to four sequential doses of her home rescue albuterol inhaler. Upon arrival via private vehicle, she was tachypneic at 30 breaths per minute, tachycardic at 118 beats per minute, hypertensive at 148/86 mmHg, and hypoxic with an oxygen saturation of 87% on room air. She was unable to speak more than two or three words consecutively without pausing for breath and was utilizing her sternocleidomastoid and scalene muscles heavily for respiration. Auscultation of the thorax revealed very tight, high-pitched expiratory wheezing throughout all lung fields with globally diminished air movement, particularly at the bases. An arterial blood gas demonstrated a respiratory alkalosis with a pCO2 of 34 mmHg; notably, a normal or rising pCO2 in a patient in acute asthma distress is an ominous sign of impending respiratory failure, requiring immediate aggressive intervention. A chest radiograph showed severe air trapping but no acute consolidation. Due to her high-risk status, Samter's triad history, and sub-optimal response to initial continuous emergency bronchodilators, she was admitted to the inpatient respiratory unit for continuous monitoring and high-dose corticosteroid management.
