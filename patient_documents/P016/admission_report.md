## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 62-year-old male with a significant medical history of chronic hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and recurrent gout who was brought to the emergency department via ambulance after experiencing the sudden onset of crushing, heavy, retrosternal chest pain while doing yard work. The patient described the pain as an elephant sitting on his chest, which radiated into his upper back and down his left arm. The chest discomfort was accompanied by profound diaphoresis, mild dyspnea, and sudden, severe lightheadedness that resulted in a near-syncopal episode, forcing him to sit down immediately. Upon assessment by paramedics, he was found to be bradycardic and hypotensive. In the emergency bay, his vital signs showed a blood pressure of 92/56 mmHg, a heart rate fluctuating between 45 and 52 beats per minute, and an oxygen saturation of 94% on 2L nasal cannula. A 12-lead ECG was emergently obtained, demonstrating striking ST-segment elevations in the inferior leads (II, III, aVF) and lateral leads (V5, V6), alongside an active conduction delay characterized by randomly dropped QRS complexes despite a constant PR interval, establishing a high-risk diagnosis of an acute inferolateral STEMI complicated by a Mobitz Type II second-degree AV block. A cardiac alert was activated, and the patient was transported directly to the cardiac catheterization suite for emergent revascularization.
