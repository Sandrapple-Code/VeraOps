## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 52-year-old female with a significant history of poorly controlled type 2 diabetes and chronic neurogenic bladder who was brought to the emergency department by emergency medical services after being found unresponsive in bed by her mother. The mother noted that the patient had been complaining of severe left flank pain, dysuria, and shaking chills for the past forty-eight hours, and had become increasingly confused and lethargic earlier that morning. Upon initial presentation in the emergency bay, the patient was profoundly hypotensive with an initial blood pressure of 74/38 mmHg, highly tachycardic with a heart rate of 128 beats per minute, febrile to 39.8 degrees Celsius, and tachypneic at 32 breaths per minute. Her Glasgow Coma Scale was evaluated at 10 (E2, V3, M5), characterized by groaning to painful stimuli and inability to follow commands. Physical examination revealed profound tenderness to percussion over the left costovertebral angle and dry, tacky mucous membranes. A central venous catheter was immediately inserted into the right internal jugular vein, and a radial arterial line was secured for continuous invasive blood pressure monitoring. Despite receiving an initial rapid bolus of 3 liters of normal saline, the patient's blood pressure remained critically low at 82/44 mmHg, satisfying the criteria for septic shock. Laboratory findings were highly critical, showing a severe leukocytosis of 24,500/uL, an acute metabolic acidosis with an unmeasured anion gap, a serum lactate of 4.8 mmol/L, and an acute kidney injury with a creatinine of 2.9 mg/dL. Urinalysis was highly indicative of an active, overwhelming infection. An urgent non-contrast CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis revealed an acute left-sided obstructive pyelonephritis caused by a 6mm stone at the ureteropelvic junction. Given the immediate life-threatening nature of an obstructed, infected collecting system combined with multi-organ failure, the Urology service was emergently consulted for operative intervention, and the patient was transferred directly to the Intensive Care Unit for resuscitation, intubation, and vasopressor titration.
