## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 55-year-old male with a history of extensive historical facial burn trauma who was brought to the emergency department as a high-priority toxicology alert after being extricated from a closed-space chemical laboratory fire explosion. Upon arrival via EMS, the patient was profoundly obtunded with a Glasgow Coma Scale of 9, tachypneic at 28 breaths per minute, tachycardic at 118 bpm, and hypotensive at 94/52 mmHg. His physical examination was highly remarkable for an unusual, bright cherry-red discoloration of his skin and mucous membranes, significant soot tracking around his nose and mouth, and a highly complex, rigid custom metallic facial mask apparatus that was carefully removed under medical supervision to facilitate airway management, revealing extensive historical full-thickness skin grafts and dense facial scarring across his left hemiface. Initial laboratory results revealed a critically toxic carboxyhemoglobin level of 32.5% combined with a profound, life-threatening metabolic lactic acidosis of 11.2 mmol/L. This severe anion gap metabolic acidosis in the setting of smoke inhalation is highly indicative of concurrent cyanide poisoning, which acts synergistically to block cellular respiration. Due to his compromised sensorium and severe inhalation toxicity, the patient was emergently intubated and transferred to the ICU for definitive antidote delivery and ventilatory support.
