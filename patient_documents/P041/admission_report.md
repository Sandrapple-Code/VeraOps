## Chief Complaint & Admission History
The patient is a 34-year-old female with a history of T6 complete paraplegia and neurogenic bladder who was admitted to the medicine service following an acute emergency room presentation for a hypertensive crisis and signs of autonomic dysreflexia. The patient reported the sudden onset of a blinding, pounding headache, severe facial flushing, drenching sweating across her face and neck, and a cold shivering sensation in her lower body, occurring shortly after she noticed her urine was exceptionally cloudy and difficult to catheterize. On physical examination in the emergency bay, she was profoundly hypertensive with a blood pressure of 185/105 mmHg (her baseline running approximately 100/60 mmHg) and bradycardic at 54 bpm, which is a classic physiological response to a noxious stimulus below the level of a spinal cord injury. Profound sweating and piloerection were noted above the chest wall, while her lower limbs were dry and cool with baseline spastic paraplegia. Palpation of the abdomen revealed a tense, distended urinary bladder, and immediate catheterization yielded 750mL of foul-smelling, turbid urine with an immediate, dramatic drops in her blood pressure. Laboratory workup confirmed a severe lower urinary tract infection, necessitating admission for definitive intravenous antibiotic therapy and monitoring against recurrent dysreflexia events.

## Initial Clinical Presentation

## Admission Orders & Treatment Plan
